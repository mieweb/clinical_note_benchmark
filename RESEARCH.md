# Research on Medical Dialogue to Clinical Note Conversion

The goal is to establish a benchmark that could be run when major model changes occur.  (ie: moving from gpt4 to gpt5 or ollama) 

## **Key Datasets**

* [**CliniKnote**](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v2) – 1,200 role-played dialogues with full SOAP notes, including entity annotations. [Github](https://github.com/catalwaysright/K-SOAP-GOODLABS)  
* [**PMC-Patients**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.13876): A Large-scale Dataset of Patient Summaries and Relations for Benchmarking Retrieval-based Clinical Decision Support Systems ([Hugginface](https://huggingface.co/datasets/AGBonnet/augmented-clinical-notes))  
  * [github repo](https://github.com/amandamarg/clinical_note_benchmark)  
* [**MedDialog**](https://papertalk.org/papertalks/11156) – Large-scale patient-doctor dialogues (Chinese \+ English). MedDialog consists of raw Q\&A-style dialogues without corresponding clinical notes, limiting its direct use for note-generation studies. gap spurred the creation of synthetic and simulated dialogue datasets that pair conversations with notes while avoiding patient privacy issues. [Github](https://github.com/yanguojun123/Medical-Dialogue)  
* [**MTS-Dialog**](https://github.com/abachaa/MTS-Dialog) – 1,700 simulated doctor–patient dialogues paired with clinical notes.  
* **I2B2 Challenges (2006–2012)** – NLP tasks on clinical text (problems, meds, treatments).  
* **MIMIC-III/IV** – Critical care dataset with clinical notes \+ structured data.

## **Note Generation Approaches**

* **MTS-Dialog baseline (Ben Abacha et al., 2023\)** – Introduced dialogue-to-note as an abstractive summarization task. Proposed fact-based evaluation (precision, recall, hallucination, omission).  
* **Summarization with Transformers (Singh et al., 2023\)** – Trained on \~900k outpatient conversations; used chain-of-facts prompting and contrastive learning.  
* **MEDIQA-Chat 2023 (Tang et al., 2023\)** – Dialogue2Note shared task. Compared fine-tuned models vs. GPT-4 in-context prompting.  
* **CliniKnote \+ K-SOAP (Li et al., 2024\)** – Added keyword extraction step before LLM summarization; improved coverage of critical details.  
* **GatorTronGPT (Lyu et al., 2024\)** – Domain-trained GPT-style model; prompt-tuning beat fine-tuned T5 on MTS-Dialog.

## **GPT Model Evaluations**

* **Kernberg et al., 2024** – Evaluated ChatGPT-4 on SOAP note generation from 14 transcripts. Found \~23.6 errors per note (86% omissions, 10.5% hallucinations). Accuracy dropped with longer transcripts.  
* **MEDIQA-Chat Results** – GPT-4 in-context learning rivaled fine-tuned models; better section structuring with classifier add-ons.  
* **Carandang et al., 2025** – Benchmarked 12 LLMs. Found LLaMA-2 70B (fine-tuned) more reliable than GPT-4 for dialogue-to-note tasks.

## **Evaluation Frameworks**

* **String Overlap:** ROUGE, BLEU.  
* **Semantic Similarity:** BERTScore, BLEURT.  
* **Fact-Based Metrics:** Fact precision/recall, hallucination & omission rates (MTS-Dialog).  
* **QA-based:** QuestEval for factual consistency.  
* **Human Expert Review:** PDQI-9 (Physician Documentation Quality Instrument).

# Converting Medical Dialogues to Clinical Notes: Datasets, Methods, and LLM Evaluation

## **Large-Scale Clinical Dialogue Datasets and Simulation**

Early work on medical dialogues established resources like **MedDialog** – a large corpus of patient-doctor conversations (with 3.4M Chinese and 0.26M English dialogues) covering dozens of diseases[\[1\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.06285v1%23:~:text=improved%2520performance%2520over%2520other%2520models,their%2520comprehensive%2520coverage%2520and%2520diversity). However, MedDialog consists of raw Q\&A-style dialogues without corresponding clinical notes, limiting its direct use for note-generation studies. This gap spurred the creation of **synthetic and simulated dialogue datasets** that pair conversations with notes while avoiding patient privacy issues[\[2\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168.pdf%23:~:text=community%2520in%2520this%2520summarization%2520task,the%2520best%2520of%2520our%2520knowledge)[\[3\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=particular,%2520we%2520introduce%2520the%2520MTS,To%2520the%2520best%2520of). For example, **MTS-Dialog** (Ben Abacha et al., 2023\) introduced *1,700* **simulated** doctor-patient dialogues each with an expert-written clinical note[\[4\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=particular,%2520we%2520introduce%2520the%2520MTS,critical%2520omissions,%2520and%2520study%2520the). MTS-Dialog was the first open benchmark for the dialogue-to-note task, although its dialogues are short snippets and the notes cover only partial sections of a full encounter note[\[5\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=is%2520insufficient%2520for%2520training%2520modern,sections%2520rather%2520than%2520full%2520notes)[\[6\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=DIALOG%25C2%25A0\(Ben%25C2%25A0Abacha%2520et%25C2%25A0al,sections%2520rather%2520than%2520full%2520notes). Other efforts have produced smaller role-play datasets – e.g. **ACI-Bench** (207 dialogues) and **PriMock57** (57 dialogues) – with complete SOAP notes, but these are too limited in scale for training modern large models[\[7\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v2%23:~:text=challenges,making)[\[8\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v2%23:~:text=Dr.Summarize%25C2%25A0,and%2520paired%2520notes%2520that%2520include). To better simulate realistic encounters, Li *et al.* (2024) created **CliniKnote**, a dataset of *1,200* **expert-roleplayed** dialogues with fully detailed clinical notes[\[9\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=field%2520of%2520clinical%2520note%2520generation,keyword%2520section%2520at%2520the%2520top). Each CliniKnote dialogue was recorded by clinicians simulating consultations and then writing the complete SOAP note for that case (ensuring no real patient data)[\[10\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=Data%2520Synthesis). Overall, these datasets provide the needed training/evaluation material for studying automated clinical note generation, filling the void left by real EHR data which cannot be shared publicly.

Automated Clinical Note Generation from Dialogue Transcripts

**Formulating Note Generation as Summarization:** Converting a multi-turn medical dialogue into a well-structured clinical note is typically approached as an *abstractive summarization* problem[\[11\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.clinicalnlp-1.18/%23:~:text=We%2520present%2520our%2520work%2520on,a%2520dataset%2520containing%2520about%2520900K). Early research applied sequence-to-sequence Transformer models with adaptations for long inputs. For instance, Singh *et al.* (2023) trained large **encoder-decoder models** on *\~900k* real outpatient conversations covering 27 specialties[\[12\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.clinicalnlp-1.18/%23:~:text=efficiency,across%2520both%2520of%2520these%2520categories). They enhanced a Transformer with a pointer-generator mechanism and techniques like multi-word tokenization, a **“chain-of-clinical-facts” prefix**, and contrastive learning over multiple candidate summaries[\[11\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.clinicalnlp-1.18/%23:~:text=We%2520present%2520our%2520work%2520on,a%2520dataset%2520containing%2520about%2520900K)[\[13\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.clinicalnlp-1.18/%23:~:text=baseline%2520model%2520which%2520include%2520using,across%2520both%2520of%2520these%2520categories). These improvements helped the model include important facts in the summary. The resulting system, evaluated on the provider’s internal dataset, showed consistent accuracy gains in generating notes for both primary care and non-primary care scenarios[\[12\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.clinicalnlp-1.18/%23:~:text=efficiency,across%2520both%2520of%2520these%2520categories).

**Fine-tuning vs. Prompting LLMs:** With the advent of large language models, researchers have compared traditional fine-tuning approaches to prompt-based strategies. In the **MEDIQA-Chat 2023** shared task (Dialogue2Note), teams explored both paradigms. For example, Tang *et al.* (2023) fine-tuned a pre-trained dialogue summarization model (and even GPT-3) on the task, and also tried few-shot **in-context learning with GPT-4**, treating the dialogue as a prompt[\[14\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=,Our%2520team%2520ranked%2520fourth%2520among). Both approaches achieved strong results – the Yale team reported ROUGE-1 F1 ≈ 0.40 and high BERTScore/BLEURT – with GPT-4 in-context generations scoring best on expert evaluation[\[14\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=,Our%2520team%2520ranked%2520fourth%2520among)[\[15\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=methods%2520achieve%2520excellent%2520results%2520in,for%2520our%2520submission%2520is%2520available). Interestingly, they also built a secondary classifier (using RoBERTa and SciBERT) to predict **section headers** for each chunk of text, ensuring the AI-generated note is properly segmented into Subjective, Objective, etc.[\[15\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=methods%2520achieve%2520excellent%2520results%2520in,for%2520our%2520submission%2520is%2520available). This reflects a trend of **structured prompting**, where the LLM is guided to produce notes in a standardized format.

**Domain-Specific LLMs:** Another line of work has leveraged specialized medical LLMs. Lyu *et al.* (2024) introduced **GatorTronGPT (20B)**, a GPT-style model trained on 277B words of clinical text. Instead of fine-tuning all model weights, they used **prompt tuning** (learned soft prompts) to teach the model to summarize dialogues[\[16\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.13089%23:~:text=generative%2520LLMs%2520to%2520summarize%2520clinical,clinical%2520ATS%2520through%2520prompt%2520tuning). In experiments on the MTS-Dialog benchmark, a prompt-tuned GatorTronGPT **outperformed a fully fine-tuned T5 model** on all metrics[\[17\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.13089%23:~:text=GatorTronGPT%2520with%2520a%2520previous%2520solution,clinical%2520ATS%2520through%2520prompt%2520tuning). Notably, the 20B LLM achieved higher overlap and fidelity scores while keeping the large model frozen (for efficiency), demonstrating that a domain-trained LLM can excel at clinical note summarization with minimal task-specific parameter updates[\[17\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.13089%23:~:text=GatorTronGPT%2520with%2520a%2520previous%2520solution,clinical%2520ATS%2520through%2520prompt%2520tuning). This result highlights the benefit of large in-domain pretraining for clinical language tasks.

**Knowledge-Augmented Pipelines:** Some research has tried to explicitly inject clinical knowledge or intermediate extraction steps to improve note generation. Li *et al.* (2024) proposed a pipeline for **K-SOAP notes**, which extends the classic SOAP format with a prepended “Keyword” section[\[18\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=networks,%2520provides%2520a%2520valuable%2520resource,performance%2520compared%2520to%2520standard%2520LLM)[\[19\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=automatic%2520pipeline%2520to%2520generate%2520K,to%2520standard%2520LLM%2520finetuning%2520methods). In their approach, after a dialogue is transcribed, a dedicated **clinical NER and relation extraction** module identifies key entities (symptoms, diagnoses, etc.) mentioned and whether they are present or absent[\[20\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=Using%2520this%2520dataset,%2520we%2520build,for%2520more%2520comprehensive%2520training%2520resources). For example, if a patient denies fever but reports cough, the keywords might include *“PRESENT: cough”* and *“ABSENT: fever”*. These structured keywords are then fed into the LLM as guiding context to generate a more accurate and comprehensive note[\[21\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v2%23:~:text=evaluating%2520models%2520in%2520clinical%2520note,modern%2520LLMs%2520using%2520various%2520metrics)[\[22\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v2%23:~:text=identification%2520of%2520essential%2520information,improvements%2520in%2520efficiency%2520and%2520performance). The authors benchmarked several modern LLMs on CliniKnote and found that this **element-aware summarization** (notes \+ keywords) improved performance over vanilla end-to-end generation[\[19\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=automatic%2520pipeline%2520to%2520generate%2520K,to%2520standard%2520LLM%2520finetuning%2520methods)[\[23\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=Furthermore,%2520we%2520finetune%2520various%2520modern,demonstrate%2520that%2520our%2520proposed%2520method). In fact, their best setting fine-tuned a medical LLaMA model with *section-specific adapters* (a lightweight tuning for each note section) and achieved state-of-the-art ROUGE and BERTScore on the CliniKnote data[\[24\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=Table%25203%2520shows%2520the%2520performance,15%2520mode%2520does%2520not%2520show)[\[25\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=BERTScores%2520among%2520all%2520open,15%2520mode%2520does%2520not%2520show). They also reported a significant reduction in note drafting time – clinicians could review and correct the AI-generated K-SOAP notes much faster than writing notes from scratch[\[19\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=automatic%2520pipeline%2520to%2520generate%2520K,to%2520standard%2520LLM%2520finetuning%2520methods)[\[26\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=investigate%2520the%2520effects%2520of%2520data,original%2520reviewing%2520and%2520writing%2520time).

**Performance of GPT-Based Models:** Given the rapid progress of GPT-3/4, researchers have evaluated these general LLMs on clinical note generation as well. On the positive side, GPT models are capable of producing well-formatted notes without special prompting. For instance, in the MEDIQA-Chat challenge, a zero-shot ChatGPT or few-shot GPT-4 could produce reasonable notes and even rival fine-tuned models[\[14\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=,Our%2520team%2520ranked%2520fourth%2520among). However, **accuracy and fidelity** remain concerns. A focused evaluation by Kernberg *et al.* (2024) used ChatGPT-4 to generate SOAP notes from 14 physician-patient encounter transcripts and had clinicians review the outputs[\[27\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Objective:%2520%2520This%2520study%2520aims,model's%2520performance%2520across%2520different%2520categories)[\[28\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=gold%2520standard%2520via%2520chart%2520review;,PDQI\)%2520scoring%2520system). They found that *although the notes were syntactically sound*, on average **23.6 errors per case** were present – **omissions** of important data made up 86% of errors, with the rest being hallucinated additions (10.5%) or incorrect details (3.2%)[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length). Notably, when the same transcript was run through ChatGPT-4 three times, there was high variability: only about **52.9% of the key data elements** were consistently captured in all three tries[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length). The “Objective” sections (e.g. vitals, exam findings) were the most accurately generated, whereas history and plan sections saw more drop-off[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length)[\[30\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=accuracy%2520observed%2520in%2520the%2520,05). Overall note quality, measured by a standard instrument (PDQI-9), fluctuated significantly between cases[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length)[\[31\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Conclusions:%2520%2520Our%2520study%2520reveals,should%2520be%2520exercised%2520before%2520widespread). The authors observed a **clear decline in accuracy for longer or more complex transcripts** (correlation with transcript length *P*≈0.05)[\[32\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=same%2520case,%2520with%2520only%252052.9,05)[\[33\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=the%2520accuracy%2520of%2520ChatGPT,05). They concluded that *GPT-4’s notes, while impressively fluent, do not yet meet the fidelity standards required for clinical use* – especially due to critical omissions and unpredictable inconsistency[\[31\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Conclusions:%2520%2520Our%2520study%2520reveals,should%2520be%2520exercised%2520before%2520widespread)[\[34\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=sections,%2520and%2520the%2520inconsistency%2520in,generated%2520clinical%2520documentation%2520at). This kind of expert audit underscores the need for careful validation of GPT-based clinical documentation before deployment.

Extracting Structured Data from Dialogues and Notes

In parallel to free-text note generation, researchers have investigated extracting **structured clinical information** (problems, medications, lab results, etc.) from dialogue transcripts or from the generated notes. One motivation is that *important facts should not be lost in summarization*. Some workflows therefore perform information extraction on the conversation as an intermediate or auxiliary task. As mentioned, CliniKnote’s system explicitly uses NER to pull out symptom and diagnosis entities and tag their **clinical status (present/absent)** in the conversation, feeding this into the note writer[\[20\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=Using%2520this%2520dataset,%2520we%2520build,for%2520more%2520comprehensive%2520training%2520resources). This ensures that the LLM is aware of every mentioned issue (including negated ones) when composing the summary. Similarly, the MEDIQA-Chat competition’s section prediction step can be seen as structuring the note by categorizing content[\[15\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=methods%2520achieve%2520excellent%2520results%2520in,for%2520our%2520submission%2520is%2520available) – effectively an extraction of which dialogue information belongs in Subjective vs. Objective parts of the note.

Even when not done explicitly, *evaluation frameworks* often convert notes and transcripts into sets of facts to measure accuracy. For example, Ben Abacha *et al.* (2023) annotated dialogues and reference notes with **semantic triples** (subject–relation–object, representing factual statements) to evaluate summarization fidelity[\[35\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=techniques,evaluations%2520of%2520clinical%2520note%2520generation). For each generated note, they computed how many facts were correctly preserved, missed, or hallucinated. If *\#reference facts* is the count of facts in the gold note and *\#system facts* in the AI note, they defined **factual precision** \= (\# of correct facts in AI note)/(\# system facts) and **factual recall** \= (\# correct facts)/(\# reference facts)[\[36\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=From%2520the%2520fact,F%2520acts%2520Hallucination%2520Rate). They also reported a **hallucination rate** (extra facts not supported by the convo) and an **omission rate** (fraction of gold facts missing)[\[37\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=Correct%2520F%2520acts%2520Reference%2520F,Correct%2520+%2520Incorrect%2520+%2520Hallucinated)[\[38\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=Hallucination%2520Rate%2520=%2520Hallucinated%2520F,the%2520normalized%2520hallucination%2520and%2520omission). In their expert-annotated sample, the AI notes often contained some hallucinated details (e.g. a wrong medication dosage) and missed some facts; these error rates were quantified to assess models’ clinical safety[\[39\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=%25E2%2580%25A2%2520Hallucinations:%2520the%2520number%2520of,the%2520number%2520of%2520incorrect%2520facts)[\[40\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=according%2520to%2520the%2520input%2520conversation,agreement%2520for%2520these%2520computations%2520are). Another team (Yim et al., 2023\) used a **key-phrase annotation** approach where any incorrect or missing clinical term in the note was marked, allowing computation of normalized hallucination and omission counts per note[\[41\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=annotators%2520were%2520required%2520to%2520write,The%2520labels%2520produced)[\[42\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=%25E2%2580%25A2%2520System%2520Facts%2520=%2520Correct,OmissionCount%2520=%2520Omitted%2520key%2520phrases). Such structured error analyses help pinpoint whether a model is, say, systematically dropping lab results or inventing symptoms.

Beyond factual accuracy, structured outputs can also include **coded data**. Some studies have suggested extracting ICD diagnostic codes or generating problem lists from the conversation, but this veers into clinical coding research. (Indeed, *MedDialog’s* Chinese version was also used for an ICD coding task[\[43\]](https://papertalk.org/papertalks/11156%23:~:text=MedDialog:%2520Large,Codes%2520to%2520Chinese%2520Clinical%2520Notes), though that involved classifying doctor-written advice, not dialogue summarization.) The general theme is that *modern approaches increasingly combine text generation with information extraction* to ensure that critical structured data – diagnoses, meds, allergies, etc. – are correctly captured. In evaluations, domain experts often specifically check for omissions of such key items (e.g. missing a critical diagnosis in the note is a severe error)[\[35\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=techniques,evaluations%2520of%2520clinical%2520note%2520generation)[\[31\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Conclusions:%2520%2520Our%2520study%2520reveals,should%2520be%2520exercised%2520before%2520widespread).

Evaluation and Benchmarking of LLMs on These Tasks

To gauge the effectiveness of models on dialogue-to-note and related tasks, researchers have employed a **blend of automated metrics and human evaluations**:

* **String Overlap Metrics:** Nearly all studies report classic metrics like ROUGE for overlap between generated notes and reference notes[\[44\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=recall%2520and%2520F1%2520score,addition%2520to%2520ROUGE,%2520we%2520introduce). In the EACL 2023 study, for instance, ROUGE was used as a baseline measure of how much of the reference note’s content is recovered[\[44\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=recall%2520and%2520F1%2520score,addition%2520to%2520ROUGE,%2520we%2520introduce). However, note-generation often requires more nuance than generic summarization, so overlap scores alone are insufficient.  
* **Embedding-Based Metrics:** Metrics such as **BERTScore** and **BLEURT** have been adopted to better capture semantic similarity[\[45\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=match%2520at%2520L414%2520more%2520advanced,uses%2520token%2520embeddings%2520to%2520compute). These use pretrained transformers to judge if the model’s note “means” the same as the gold note, even if phrased differently. Li *et al.* (2024) used BERTScore and BLEURT (with RoBERTa-large and BLEURT-20) to evaluate their K-SOAP notes, finding these metrics more sensitive to clinical relevance than ROUGE[\[45\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=match%2520at%2520L414%2520more%2520advanced,uses%2520token%2520embeddings%2520to%2520compute)[\[46\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=text,%2520offering%2520insights%2520into%2520the,20). In MEDIQA-Chat, top teams also optimized for BERTScore and BLEURT, which contributed to higher placements[\[47\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05001%23:~:text=shared%2520task,%2520encompassing%2520both%2520subtask,Our%2520team%2520ranked%2520fourth%2520among).  
* **Fact-specific Metrics:** As discussed, fact extraction can yield precision/recall scores for clinical facts[\[36\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-acl.161.pdf%23:~:text=From%2520the%2520fact,F%2520acts%2520Hallucination%2520Rate). Another approach is **question-answering evaluation** – e.g. **QuestEval**, which generates questions from the reference note and checks if the model’s note can answer them[\[48\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=framework,answering%2520models). Li *et al.* used QuestEval to assess factual consistency and reported it had much better correlation with human judgments on note **consistency, coherence, fluency, and relevance** than ROUGE[\[48\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14568v1%23:~:text=framework,answering%2520models). This aligns with other findings that QA-based metrics help detect clinical hallucinations or omissions (for example, a question like “What medication is the patient on?” would reveal if the model missed a medication that was in the transcript).  
* **Human Expert Evaluation:** Ultimately, human judgment is the gold standard for clinical relevance. Many studies included **physician reviews** of generated notes. Experts often use rubrics from medical documentation research – for example, the **Physician Documentation Quality Instrument (PDQI-9)**, which scores notes on dimensions like completeness, clarity, and usefulness. Kernberg *et al.* applied PDQI-9 to ChatGPT-4 notes and found significant variance, echoing the inconsistency in content accuracy[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length)[\[49\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Conclusions:%2520%2520Our%2520study%2520reveals,to%2520address%2520accuracy,%2520variability,%2520and). Another common focus is **error categorization**: experts manually label **omissions, incorrect additions,** and any **clinical inaccuracies**, as seen in multiple studies[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length)[\[50\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=note%2520quality%2520generated%2520by%2520ChatGPT,in%2520various%2520applications,%2520should%2520not). Ben Abacha *et al.* specifically had clinicians note “critical omissions” – missing information that would adversely affect care – in each summary[\[35\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=techniques,evaluations%2520of%2520clinical%2520note%2520generation). This revealed cases where an AI left out crucial findings (e.g. a test result or a key symptom), which automatic metrics might not fully penalize. The correlation analysis in that work showed that certain metrics (like fact recall or QuestEval) better predicted expert satisfaction than surface metrics did[\[35\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=techniques,evaluations%2520of%2520clinical%2520note%2520generation).  
* **Head-to-Head Model Comparisons:** Recent research has also benchmarked multiple LLMs on the same note-generation task. A 2025 study by Carandang *et al.* evaluated **12 different LLMs** (4 proprietary services and 8 open-source models) on generating notes from dialogue transcripts[\[51\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17095v1%23:~:text=In%2520this%2520study,%2520we%2520sought,incorporation%2520into%2520real%2520clinical%2520workflows)[\[52\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17095v1%23:~:text=same%2520prompt%2520in%2520multiple%2520iterations,incorporation%2520into%2520real%2520clinical%2520workflows). They assessed both **correctness** (against reference notes) and **reliability** (consistency across runs). Interestingly, an **open-source model** (Meta’s LLaMA-2 70B, fine-tuned for the task) was rated *most reliable* – producing notes closest to expert references with minimal variability[\[53\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17095v1%23:~:text=Overall,%2520Meta%25E2%2580%2599s%2520Llama%252070B%2520was,efficiency%2520of%2520HCPs%2520in%2520clinical). It slightly outperformed even GPT-4 in their tests. A smaller open model (Mistral 7B) was the next best in reliability[\[53\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17095v1%23:~:text=Overall,%2520Meta%25E2%2580%2599s%2520Llama%252070B%2520was,efficiency%2520of%2520HCPs%2520in%2520clinical). The authors suggest that deploying such *locally hosted, fine-tuned models* may be preferable in clinical settings, both for data privacy and because they can be optimized to be more stable in their output[\[53\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17095v1%23:~:text=Overall,%2520Meta%25E2%2580%2599s%2520Llama%252070B%2520was,efficiency%2520of%2520HCPs%2520in%2520clinical). This kind of benchmark underscores that while GPT-4 is very capable, domain-specific tuning of open models can narrow the gap and even excel on certain facets like consistency. It also reflects the importance of evaluating *not just average performance, but reliability and worst-case errors* when considering LLMs for clinical note-taking.

In summary, the field has made rapid progress by leveraging large medical dialogue datasets and powerful LLMs to automatically produce clinical notes from transcripts. Researchers have combined free-text generation with structured data extraction to ensure important details (diagnoses, medications, labs, etc.) are retained in the notes. Across numerous studies and benchmarks – from fine-tuned sequence-to-sequence models on huge provider datasets to GPT-4 prompt-based methods – there is a trend of improving **accuracy, brevity, and clinical fidelity** of generated notes. Evaluation frameworks now go beyond ROUGE, employing embedding-based semantic metrics, fact-level checks, and rigorous human review to measure **factual correctness and clinical relevance**[\[35\]](https://aclanthology.org/2023.eacl-main.168/%23:~:text=techniques,evaluations%2520of%2520clinical%2520note%2520generation)[\[31\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Conclusions:%2520%2520Our%2520study%2520reveals,should%2520be%2520exercised%2520before%2520widespread). Key benchmark results show that dedicated clinical LLMs (like GatorTronGPT) and carefully fine-tuned open models can rival or surpass general models on these tasks[\[17\]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.13089%23:~:text=GatorTronGPT%2520with%2520a%2520previous%2520solution,clinical%2520ATS%2520through%2520prompt%2520tuning)[\[53\]](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.17095v1%23:~:text=Overall,%2520Meta%25E2%2580%2599s%2520Llama%252070B%2520was,efficiency%2520of%2520HCPs%2520in%2520clinical). Yet, studies also highlight challenges: e.g. GPT-4 may generate impressively fluent notes but omit critical details unpredictably[\[29\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=Results:%2520%2520Although%2520ChatGPT,to%2520both%2520the%2520transcript%2520length)[\[50\]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38648636/%23:~:text=note%2520quality%2520generated%2520by%2520ChatGPT,in%2520various%2520applications,%2520should%2520not). Going forward, ensuring **faithfulness and reliability** – perhaps via hybrid extraction-generation methods and thorough validation – remains the central concern in turning medical dialogues into safe, effective clinical documentation.
